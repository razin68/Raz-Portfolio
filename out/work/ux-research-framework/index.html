Improving Provider Experience

Redesigning provider search and profiles, with a weighted-scoring research framework deciding which changes shipped, and which to kill.

Project Overview

Outcomes

  • Increased provider search comprehension by 275%
  • Shipped two provider-search changes; killed one before a sprint
  • Built reusable research infrastructure that now powers other studies

The Problem

Patients struggled to find and evaluate the right provider, and the team had no reliable way to tell which fixes would actually help, so changes were made on opinion.

The Solution

I redesigned provider search and profiles through three tested hypotheses, powered by a weighted-scoring research framework (Sentiment × Severity) I built to decide what shipped. Two changes shipped; one was killed before a sprint was spent.

The Starting Point

GoalMake research produce answers leadership would act on

When I joined the web team at an academic medical center, design decisions ran on opinion. Research happened, but nothing turned it into proof a roadmap would move for. Everyone agreed research mattered. Proving a hypothesis well enough to change direction was the part nobody had solved. Three things kept getting in the way.

The provider search redesign was the first real test, and the reason the framework exists. The team kept proposing fixes nobody could prove. I needed to validate or reject each assumption with evidence, not opinion. Old research handed over themes and quotes: easy to argue with, impossible to rank. The framework turns each session into a number instead. WEIGHTED IMPACT SCORE = Sentiment × Severity Sentiment is direction, from −3 to +3: did this help the user or hurt them? I read it from the whole interview, tone, expression, body language, where someone hesitates, not just the words. Severity is how much it mattered, from 1 to 3. Multiply them and every observation carries one weighted score, rolled up to the hypothesis it tested.

  1. 01

    Observation Logging

    Every user behavior, reaction, and comment is logged as a discrete observation.

  2. 02

    Taxonomy Coding

    Observations are tagged against a 27-code taxonomy to cut interpretation drift between studies.

  3. 03

    Severity Scoring

    Each observation gets a sentiment score (−3 to +3) and a severity score (1 to 3), multiplied into a weighted impact score.

  4. 04

    Hypothesis Analysis

    Scores roll up to the hypothesis level to compare baseline usability, proposed changes, and observed behavior.

03

Three Hypotheses, Three Verdicts

GoalShip what the evidence supports, kill what it doesn't

With the framework running, I tested three design hypotheses against a documented baseline, each across 6 moderated user interviews. Every hypothesis produced a numeric Efficacy Delta that went straight to the roadmap: two to build, one to kill before a sprint was spent.

+0Δ

sentiment swing across 6 interviews (−2 → +15)

H1

Specialties Label

Shipped

Hypothesis: a clear "Specialties" label on the provider profile header helps patients see what a provider actually treats. It landed. Across 6 interviews, sentiment swung from −2 (skimming past key information, unsure what the provider did) to +15 (immediately engaged with the provider's experience): a 275% lift in comprehension. Shipped.

+0.0%

filter engagement, across 6 interviews plus analytics

H2

Filter Shift

Shipped

Hypothesis: renaming the filter to match how patients actually talk, and opening it by default, drives more use. The dashboard loved it: +18.6% clicks. But across 6 interviews, sentiment only nudged to +2. I triangulated both signals in Looker Studio and presented one unified read that caught a false positive the analytics alone would have called a win. Shipped, with eyes open. The divergence was the finding.

−0Δ

sentiment across 6 interviews

H3

Connected Profiles

Killed

Hypothesis: surfacing a provider's published research on their profile card builds patient trust. It was already approved for development. Across 6 interviews it scored −21. People didn't click the widget, and it created confusion that actively undermined trust. The framework killed it before engineering spent a single sprint.

Operational Impact

GoalMake prior research reusable instead of disposable

The framework's real payoff showed up after the provider study. Because every session is scored and stored in one relational base, old research stops being a finished report and becomes data I can query again. So I ran the framework backwards over interviews from earlier projects: same sessions, sharper lens, defensible new insight out of work everyone had already filed away.

05

Key Learnings

Whether you're building a platform for construction businesses or a healthcare product at institutional scale, the problem is the same: design decisions get made on opinion until you build the infrastructure to make them on evidence.

The right direction

Every product team has a feature someone is confident about that users don't need. A scored framework catches it before engineering spends a sprint.

Decisions that stick

When leadership can see a number, not just a theme or a quote, research becomes a direction mechanism. Buy-in gets easier to win and harder to reverse.

Data that compounds

A relational research architecture means every study makes the next one smarter. You add to a system with institutional memory instead of starting from zero.

My Impact

Design decisions get made on opinion until you have the infrastructure to make them on evidence. I've built that infrastructure, and it works at any scale.
